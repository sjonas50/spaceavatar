## How stars live and die
- Stars are born in collapsing clouds of gas and dust called nebulae; the Orion Nebula is a stellar nursery visible to the naked eye.
- A star's mass decides its fate: our Sun will swell into a red giant in about 5 billion years, then fade as a white dwarf.
- Stars much heavier than the Sun end in supernova explosions that briefly outshine entire galaxies — and forge the heavy elements. The calcium in your bones and iron in your blood were made inside stars: we are, literally, made of stardust.
- A supernova seen in 1054 left behind the Crab Nebula; Chinese astronomers recorded the "guest star" that was visible in daytime for weeks.

## Neutron stars and black holes
- A neutron star packs about 1.4 Suns into a city-sized sphere ~20 km across; a sugar-cube of its material would weigh about a billion tonnes.
- Pulsars are spinning neutron stars whose radio beams sweep past Earth like lighthouse flashes — some spin hundreds of times per second.
- A black hole's gravity is so strong that not even light escapes past its event horizon.
- The Event Horizon Telescope produced the first image of a black hole (galaxy M87's) in 2019, and imaged Sagittarius A* — the 4-million-solar-mass black hole at our galaxy's center — in 2022.
- In 2015, LIGO detected gravitational waves — ripples in spacetime from two black holes colliding over a billion light-years away — confirming Einstein's century-old prediction.

## Galaxies and the universe
- The Milky Way is a barred spiral roughly 100,000 light-years across with 100–400 billion stars.
- The Andromeda galaxy, 2.5 million light-years away, is heading toward us; the two galaxies will merge in about 4.5 billion years (stars are so far apart that collisions between them are unlikely).
- The observable universe is about 13.8 billion years old, and its afterglow — the cosmic microwave background — is still detectable in every direction.
- The universe's expansion is accelerating, driven by something we call dark energy; together dark energy and dark matter make up about 95% of the universe, and we don't yet know what either is.

## Great telescopes
- The Hubble Space Telescope, launched in 1990, was famously nearsighted until astronauts installed corrective optics in 1993 — five shuttle servicing missions kept it a scientific giant for decades.
- The James Webb Space Telescope launched on Christmas Day 2021, unfolded a tennis-court-sized sunshield, and observes from 1.5 million km away. Its infrared eyes see the universe's earliest galaxies and analyze the atmospheres of planets around other stars.
- Hubble's "Deep Field" images showed thousands of galaxies in a patch of sky the size of a pinhead held at arm's length.

## Planets around other stars
- The first exoplanet around a Sun-like star was confirmed in 1995; thousands are now known, many found by NASA's Kepler and TESS space telescopes watching for tiny dips in starlight.
- Known worlds include "hot Jupiters" that orbit in days, planets orbiting two stars, and rocky planets in their star's habitable zone — where liquid water could exist.
- The TRAPPIST-1 system, 40 light-years away, packs seven roughly Earth-sized planets around one small red star.
