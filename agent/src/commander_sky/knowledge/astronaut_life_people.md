## Living in orbit
- There's gravity in orbit — about 90% of Earth's. Astronauts float because they're in continuous free fall around the planet, falling *with* their spacecraft.
- Without exercise, astronauts can lose 1–2% of bone density per month; the ISS gym (treadmill, bike, and a resistance machine) is two hours of every day.
- Sleep happens in wall-strapped bags; some astronauts love it, some miss pillows. Eyes see occasional light flashes — cosmic rays passing through.
- Water is recycled aggressively on the ISS — including from sweat and urine. Astronauts joke that "yesterday's coffee becomes tomorrow's coffee."
- No crying like on Earth: tears don't fall, they just pool around your eyes.
- Returning astronauts describe the "overview effect" — a profound shift from seeing Earth as one thin-atmosphered, borderless world.

## Spacewalks
- A spacewalk (EVA) typically lasts 6–8 hours; suits are essentially one-person spacecraft with oxygen, cooling water, and micrometeoroid armor.
- Astronauts train for spacewalks underwater in NASA's Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory — a pool holding 23 million liters with a full-size ISS mockup.
- Everything is tethered — astronaut and tools alike. Suits carry a small jetpack (SAFER) as a last-resort ride home if a tether fails.
- In 2013, ESA astronaut Luca Parmitano's helmet began filling with water from a suit leak; he calmly navigated back by feel, and suits gained snorkels and absorbent pads afterward.

## Becoming an astronaut
- NASA astronaut selections are extraordinarily competitive — recent classes have drawn over 12,000 applicants for roughly ten spots.
- Typical requirements: a STEM master's degree plus years of professional or flight experience, and passing a demanding physical.
- Training takes about two years and includes robotics, spacewalk skills, Russian language, T-38 jet flight, and wilderness survival.
- Astronauts come from many paths: military pilots, doctors, geologists, oceanographers, teachers, and engineers.

## Pioneers worth knowing
- Sally Ride (first American woman in space, 1983) later founded a science-education company inspiring girls in STEM.
- Guion Bluford became the first African American in space in 1983; Mae Jemison, the first African American woman, flew in 1992 — she took a poster from Alvin Ailey's dance company with her.
- Chris Hadfield, Canada's most famous astronaut, commanded the ISS and made space feel human through videos — including how to wring out a wet towel in zero-g.
- Peggy Whitson has commanded the ISS twice and holds the American record for cumulative time in space — over 675 days including her later commercial missions.
- Frank Rubio's 371-day mission (2022–2023) — extended after his ride home developed a coolant leak — set the single-flight American record.
- Katherine Johnson, celebrated in "Hidden Figures," calculated trajectories for Mercury and Apollo; she received the Presidential Medal of Freedom at age 97.
- John Young flew Gemini, Apollo (walking on the Moon on Apollo 16), and commanded the first space shuttle flight — the only person to fly all three.
