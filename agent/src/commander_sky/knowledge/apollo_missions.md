## Apollo 1
- January 27, 1967: a fire during a launch-pad test killed astronauts Gus Grissom, Ed White, and Roger Chaffee.
- The tragedy led to a complete redesign of the command module — a new hatch that opened outward in seconds, fireproof materials, and a safer cabin atmosphere on the pad.
- NASA's flight director Gene Kranz told his team afterward that mission control would forever be "tough and competent" — words still taught at NASA today.

## Apollo 7
- October 1968: the first crewed Apollo flight, commanded by Wally Schirra, tested the redesigned command module in Earth orbit for 11 days.
- It included the first live television broadcast from an American spacecraft.

## Apollo 8
- December 1968: Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and Bill Anders became the first humans to leave Earth's gravity and orbit the Moon — ten orbits on Christmas Eve.
- Bill Anders took the famous "Earthrise" photograph.
- The crew read from the Book of Genesis in a Christmas Eve broadcast watched by an estimated billion people.

## Apollo 9 and Apollo 10
- Apollo 9 (March 1969) tested the lunar module in Earth orbit — the first crewed flight of the spidery lander.
- Apollo 10 (May 1969) was the full dress rehearsal: Tom Stafford and Gene Cernan flew the lunar module "Snoopy" to within 15 kilometers of the Moon's surface without landing. The command module was called "Charlie Brown."

## Apollo 11
- July 16–24, 1969: Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins flew the first Moon landing mission.
- Eagle landed in the Sea of Tranquility on July 20, 1969, with about 25 seconds of fuel margin remaining after Armstrong flew past a boulder field.
- During descent, the guidance computer flashed 1202 and 1201 program alarms; 26-year-old engineer Steve Bales made the call that it was safe to continue.
- Armstrong and Aldrin spent about 21.5 hours on the surface and returned 21.5 kg of samples.

## Apollo 12
- November 1969: Pete Conrad and Alan Bean made a pinpoint landing next to the Surveyor 3 probe, which had landed two years earlier — proving precision landings were possible.
- The Saturn V was struck by lightning twice during launch; the mission continued after the famous call "SCE to AUX" restored telemetry.

## Apollo 13
- April 1970: an oxygen tank exploded 330,000 km from Earth. Jim Lovell, Jack Swigert, and Fred Haise survived by using the lunar module Aquarius as a lifeboat.
- The actual radio call was "Houston, we've had a problem" (the movie changed the tense).
- Mission control improvised a carbon-dioxide scrubber adapter out of plastic bags, cardboard, and tape — astronauts built it from instructions read over the radio. All three came home safely.

## Apollo 14
- February 1971: Alan Shepard, America's first astronaut, walked on the Moon at age 47 and hit two golf balls with a makeshift club.
- Crewmate Stuart Roosa carried tree seeds that were later planted across America — they're known as "Moon Trees."

## Apollo 15, 16, and 17
- These "J missions" (1971–1972) carried the electric lunar rover and stayed on the Moon for about three days each.
- Apollo 15's Dave Scott dropped a hammer and a falcon feather together on live TV — they hit the ground at the same time, proving Galileo right about gravity.
- Apollo 17 (December 1972) carried Harrison "Jack" Schmitt, the only geologist to walk on the Moon. Gene Cernan was the last human to leave lunar footprints; his final words on the surface honored returning "with peace and hope for all mankind."
