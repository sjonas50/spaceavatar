## How rockets work
- A rocket works by Newton's third law: throw mass out the back fast, and the rocket goes forward — it needs nothing to push against, which is why it works in vacuum.
- Reaching orbit is less about going up than going sideways: about 28,000 km/h horizontally, so you keep "falling around" the Earth.
- Most of a rocket is fuel — typically 85–90% of its weight at liftoff.
- The Saturn V's five F-1 engines burned about 15 tonnes of fuel per second at liftoff and produced 34.5 million newtons of thrust; it remains the most powerful rocket ever flown successfully until recent years.

## Reusable rockets
- SpaceX's Falcon 9 changed launch economics by landing its first stage — first successful landing in December 2015, with individual boosters since reflying 20+ times.
- Boosters land by relighting engines and touching down on landing legs, either back at the launch site or on autonomous drone ships with names like "Of Course I Still Love You."
- SpaceX's Starship — the largest rocket ever built, about 120 meters tall — has been in flight testing through the mid-2020s, aiming for full reusability of both stages. Its Super Heavy booster has been caught back at the launch tower by mechanical arms.

## The Artemis program
- Artemis is NASA's program to return humans to the Moon, with international and commercial partners.
- Artemis I (late 2022) flew the uncrewed Orion capsule around the Moon on the SLS rocket and returned safely.
- Artemis II is planned to carry four astronauts — including the first woman and first Canadian on a lunar mission — around the Moon; as of this archive's mid-2026 update it was scheduled for the near term, so current status should be checked.
- Later Artemis missions plan to land crews near the lunar south pole, where permanently shadowed craters hold water ice — potential drinking water, air, and rocket fuel.
- A planned small station called Gateway would orbit the Moon as a staging point.

## Commercial spaceflight
- SpaceX's Crew Dragon has carried NASA astronauts since 2020 — the first crewed orbital launches from U.S. soil since the shuttle retired in 2011.
- Private crews have flown to orbit and to the ISS; Inspiration4 (2021) was the first all-civilian orbital mission.
- Suborbital tourists have flown with Blue Origin and Virgin Galactic on minutes-long hops past the edge of space.
- The Kármán line, 100 km up, is the most-used boundary of "space" — though the atmosphere fades gradually, not at a line.

## Deep-space probes and planetary defense
- NASA's DART mission deliberately crashed into the asteroid moonlet Dimorphos in 2022 and measurably changed its orbit — the first planetary-defense test.
- OSIRIS-REx returned samples of asteroid Bennu to Earth in 2023; Japan's Hayabusa2 returned samples of asteroid Ryugu in 2020.
- The Parker Solar Probe has flown through the Sun's outer atmosphere — the fastest human-made object ever, over 600,000 km/h at closest approach.
