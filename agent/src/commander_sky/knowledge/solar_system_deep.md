## Mercury and Venus
- Mercury's temperature swings are the solar system's wildest: about 430°C in daytime and −180°C at night, because it has almost no atmosphere to hold heat.
- A day on Mercury (sunrise to sunrise) lasts about 176 Earth days — longer than its 88-day year.
- Venus's surface pressure is about 90 times Earth's — like being 900 meters underwater — and its clouds are sulfuric acid.
- Soviet Venera landers reached Venus's surface in the 1970s–80s; the longest survivor lasted about two hours before the heat destroyed it.

## Mars exploration
- More than half of all Mars missions ever attempted have failed — engineers call it the "Mars curse."
- NASA's rovers in order: Sojourner (1997), Spirit and Opportunity (2004), Curiosity (2012), Perseverance (2021). Opportunity was designed for 90 days and drove for nearly 15 years.
- Perseverance carried Ingenuity, a 1.8-kg helicopter that made the first powered flight on another planet in April 2021 and flew dozens of flights over three years.
- Mars has seasons, polar ice caps, ancient river deltas, and dust storms that can wrap the whole planet.
- A one-way radio signal to Mars takes between about 4 and 24 minutes depending on orbital positions — rovers must handle themselves between commands.

## Jupiter and its moons
- Jupiter is heavier than all the other planets combined — 318 times Earth's mass.
- Its four largest moons, found by Galileo in 1610, are worlds of their own: volcanic Io (the most volcanically active body known), icy Europa, giant Ganymede, and cratered Callisto.
- Europa almost certainly hides a saltwater ocean beneath its ice shell with more water than all Earth's oceans — a prime target in the search for life. NASA's Europa Clipper launched in October 2024 to study it with dozens of close flybys.

## Saturn and its moons
- Saturn's rings span about 280,000 km but are typically only around 10 meters thick — proportionally thinner than a sheet of paper.
- Titan, Saturn's largest moon, has rivers and lakes of liquid methane and a thicker atmosphere than Earth's. ESA's Huygens probe landed there in 2005 — the most distant landing ever made.
- Tiny Enceladus shoots geysers of water ice from its south pole; Cassini flew through the plumes and found salt and organic molecules from its buried ocean.
- NASA's Dragonfly mission plans to fly a nuclear-powered rotorcraft on Titan in the 2030s.

## Ice giants, dwarf planets, and the edge
- Uranus and Neptune have each been visited exactly once — by Voyager 2, in 1986 and 1989.
- Pluto, reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006, turned out to be spectacular when New Horizons flew past in 2015: a heart-shaped nitrogen glacier, ice mountains, and a thin blue haze.
- The Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune holds icy worlds; far beyond that, the theorized Oort Cloud is where long-period comets come from.
- Voyager 1 and 2, launched in 1977, have left the Sun's protective bubble and are in interstellar space — still whispering data home. Each carries a Golden Record of Earth's sounds and greetings.
