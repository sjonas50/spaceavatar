# Spaceflight Firsts

- Yuri Gagarin became the first human in space on April 12, 1961, orbiting Earth
  once aboard Vostok 1.
- Alan Shepard became the first American in space a few weeks later, on May 5, 1961.
  He later walked on the Moon on Apollo 14 — and hit two golf balls there.
- Valentina Tereshkova became the first woman in space in 1963.
- Sally Ride became the first American woman in space in 1983.
- Katherine Johnson, a NASA mathematician, calculated launch and return
  trajectories for the Mercury and Apollo missions by hand — John Glenn asked for
  her personally to check the computer's numbers before his orbital flight.

# The International Space Station

- The ISS orbits about 400 kilometers (250 miles) above Earth, traveling around
  28,000 kilometers per hour — it circles the whole planet in about 90 minutes.
- Astronauts aboard see about 16 sunrises and sunsets every day.
- People have lived on the ISS continuously since November 2000.

# Life and Records in Space

- Astronauts get a little taller in space — up to about 5 centimeters (2 inches) —
  because their spines stretch out without gravity. It reverses back on Earth.
- Without exercise, muscles and bones weaken in weightlessness, so astronauts work
  out about two hours every day.
- Astronauts eat tortillas instead of bread, because crumbs float everywhere. Salt
  and pepper come as liquids for the same reason.
- Valeri Polyakov holds the record for the longest single spaceflight: 437 days
  aboard the Mir station.
- Frank Rubio holds the American record for a single spaceflight: 371 days,
  returning in 2023.
- Peggy Whitson has spent more time in space than any other American — more than
  665 days across her missions.
- Chris Hadfield recorded a music video of David Bowie's "Space Oddity" aboard the
  ISS in 2013 — the first music video filmed in space.
