# The Sun

- The Sun is a star — an average-sized one called a yellow dwarf, about 4.6 billion
  years old.
- It holds about 99.8 percent of all the mass in the solar system; roughly 1.3
  million Earths could fit inside it.
- Sunlight takes about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth.
- The Sun's surface is around 5,500 degrees Celsius (about 10,000 degrees Fahrenheit).

# The Planets

- There are eight planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus,
  and Neptune. Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006.
- Mercury is the smallest planet and orbits the Sun fastest — its year is just 88
  Earth days — but it is not the hottest planet.
- Venus is the hottest planet, around 465 degrees Celsius, because its thick
  atmosphere traps heat. It spins backwards, and its day is longer than its year.
- Mars is red because of iron oxide — rust — in its soil. It has the tallest
  volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, about two and a half times the height
  of Mount Everest, and two small moons, Phobos and Deimos.
- Jupiter is the largest planet. Its Great Red Spot is a storm bigger than Earth
  that has raged for centuries. Its moon Ganymede is the largest moon in the solar
  system — bigger than the planet Mercury.
- Saturn's rings are mostly chunks of water ice, from dust-sized to house-sized.
  Saturn is less dense than water. Its moon Titan has a thick atmosphere and lakes
  of liquid methane.
- Uranus rotates on its side, likely knocked over by an ancient collision.
- Neptune has the strongest winds in the solar system — up to about 2,000
  kilometers per hour — and was found in 1846 by mathematical prediction before
  anyone saw it through a telescope.

# Asteroids and Comets

- The asteroid belt lies between Mars and Jupiter. Despite the movies, asteroids
  there are usually millions of kilometers apart.
- Comets are ancient balls of ice and dust. When they near the Sun they grow tails —
  and the tail always points away from the Sun, pushed by sunlight and solar wind.

# Stars and Galaxies

- Stars come in colors that reveal their temperature: blue stars are the hottest,
  red stars the coolest. Our yellow Sun is in between.
- Stars twinkle only because Earth's moving air bends their light — from space,
  they shine steadily.
- A light-year is the distance light travels in one year — about 9.5 trillion
  kilometers.
- The nearest star system to ours is Alpha Centauri, a little over four light-years
  away.
- Our galaxy, the Milky Way, holds more than 100 billion stars. The Sun takes
  roughly 230 million years to orbit its center once.
- Betelgeuse, a red supergiant in Orion, is so large that placed where the Sun is,
  it would swallow the orbit of Mars.
- A neutron star is so dense that a single teaspoon of it would weigh billions of
  tons.
- The night sky is divided into 88 official constellations, and Polaris — the North
  Star — sits almost directly above Earth's North Pole.
